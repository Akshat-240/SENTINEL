REFERENCE NOTE: Common Patterns Across Indian Industrial Incidents
(Compiled for RAG knowledge base - general safety learning reference)

Across publicly documented Indian industrial incidents involving gas
leaks, fires, and confined space hazards, several recurring patterns
emerge that are useful for automated risk detection systems:

1. COMPOUND PERMIT RISK: A large share of serious incidents involve two or
   more simultaneously active permits in the same zone (most commonly Hot
   Work overlapping with Confined Space entry). Systems that only check one
   permit at a time miss this compounding danger.

2. NIGHT SHIFT VULNERABILITY: Incidents during night shifts tend to have
   fewer supervisory staff present and slower response times, even when
   the underlying hazard (gas leak, fire risk) is similar in nature to a
   day-shift scenario.

3. RATE OF CHANGE MATTERS MORE THAN ABSOLUTE VALUE: Sharp, fast-rising gas
   trends (e.g., 20 PPM to 800+ PPM within 10 minutes) are associated with
   worse outcomes than slow, moderate rises, largely because slow rises
   leave more time for manual or automated intervention.

4. HISTORICAL ZONE RISK IS PREDICTIVE: Zones with a prior history of minor
   leaks or near-misses are statistically more likely to experience a
   repeat or escalated incident, supporting the use of a "zone history
   score" as an input to a compound risk model.

5. THE INTERVENTION WINDOW: Near-miss cases (like Jamshedpur, 2021) show
   that a window of roughly 1-6 hours often exists between early warning
   signs and a critical threshold breach. Automated systems that can
   detect and alert within this window have a realistic chance of
   preventing escalation to a fatal outcome.
